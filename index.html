    Overview

    Click to edit text

    While the U.S. government taxes millions of working minors every year, it denies those same individuals the most basic democratic right: representation. This section breaks down the key constitutional violations, legal contradictions, and IRS code sections that support our argument: No Taxation Without Representation isnt just a slogan its a legal principle.

    1. Equal Protection Clause 14th Amendment

    No State shall... deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws. U.S. Constitution, Amendment XIV, 1

    Argument:
     Minors are taxed the same as adults, but are not treated equally under the law. They cannot vote, cannot serve on juries, and cannot influence legislation yet they are forced to contribute to the very systems they have no control over. That is unequal protection.


    Click to edit text

    2. Due Process Clause 5th & 14th Amendments

    No person shall be... deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law.

    Argument:
     Taxation is the seizure of income (property). For minors, this occurs without full legal recognition or civic rights. This contradicts the idea that law must treat people fairly and with a voice in decisions affecting them the core of due process.

    3. Internal Revenue Code Title 26 U.S. Code

    63(c)(5): Standard Deduction for Dependents

    Limits deductions for minors, despite taxing them individually.
     Effect: Treats them as "lesser" taxpayers.

    1(g): Kiddie Tax

    Taxes unearned income of minors at their parents' higher tax rates.
     Effect: Targets minors explicitly while denying them adult rights.

    152(c): Dependent Definition

    Minors are dependents by law, not fully autonomous.
     Effect: Confirms that minors lack adult status yet are taxed as if they had it.

    6103: Return Access

    Parents can access their minor childs tax info.
     Effect: Confirms limited legal autonomy for minors.

    4. Historical Precedent: No Taxation Without Representation

    Originating from colonial resistance to British rule, this principle is now part of American legal and moral identity. Denying political representation while collecting taxes was a key reason the U.S. declared independence. Today, that same injustice is applied to tax-paying minors.

    5. Legal Contradiction

    Minors are:

    • Denied the vote (15th, 19th, 26th Amendments),

    • Taxed on wages as early as age 14,

    • Legally defined as dependents (not full citizens),

    • Yet treated as full taxpayers under Title 26 U.S.C.

    Conclusion: You can't legally or ethically treat someone like a citizen only when it benefits the government.


    What We Want

    We are not asking for special treatment. We are asking for constitutional consistency:

    • End the taxation of minors, or

    • Grant them full democratic rights.

    Until then, taxing working minors violates the most basic principles of American law.

    Oregon House Bill 5017 The Youth Fair Taxation Act

    Sponsored by: Representative [TBD], Senator [TBD]
    Introduced: [TBD]
    Referred to: House Committee on Revenue


    A BILL FOR AN ACT

    Relating to the taxation of employed minors; creating new provisions; amending ORS Chapters 316 and 305.


    Be It Enacted by the People of the State of Oregon:


    SECTION 1. Short Title.
    This Act shall be known and may be cited as the "Youth Fair Taxation Act."


    SECTION 2. Legislative Findings.
    (1) The Legislative Assembly finds that:

    (a) The State of Oregon currently imposes income tax on working individuals regardless of age, including unemancipated minors under the age of 18.

    (b) These minors, while subject to the same income taxation as legal adults, are not granted the right to vote, serve on juries, run for elected office, or exercise full legal autonomy in Oregon.

    (c) This contradiction constitutes a form of taxation without representation, which is antithetical to the principles established by the founding documents of the United States and the State of Oregon.

    (d) The Fourteenth Amendment of the U.S. Constitution guarantees equal protection under the law, and imposing financial obligations on a politically disenfranchised group undermines this protection.

    (e) Oregon has a moral and constitutional obligation to ensure that any class of taxed individuals be afforded fair political representation or be exempted from taxation until such representation is granted.


    SECTION 3. Definitions.
    As used in this Act:

    (1) "Minor" means any individual under the age of 18 who has not been emancipated pursuant to ORS 419B.550 to 419B.558.

    (2) "Earned income" means wages, salaries, commissions, tips, and other compensation received for services performed.

    (3) "Unemancipated minor" means a minor who is under the legal custody of a parent or guardian and is not recognized as having adult legal status.


    SECTION 4. Income Tax Exemption for Minors.
    (1) Notwithstanding any provision of ORS Chapter 316 to the contrary, no state income tax shall be imposed on the earned income of any unemancipated minor under the age of 18.

    (2) The exemption shall apply to taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2026.

    (3) The Oregon Department of Revenue shall revise any relevant forms, withholding tables, and guidelines to reflect this exemption.

    (4) The exemption shall not apply to passive income such as dividends, interest, or trust distributions unless earned directly through work performed by the minor.


    SECTION 5. Rulemaking.
    The Oregon Department of Revenue shall adopt all necessary rules to implement this Act no later than October 1, 2025. The department shall ensure:

    (1) That employers are informed of the exemption and updated withholding requirements.

    (2) That minors, guardians, tax preparers, and financial institutions are adequately educated about the change.

    (3) That any tax filings received from ineligible minors in violation of this Act are returned or adjusted with notice.


    SECTION 6. Review and Reporting Requirements.
    (1) The Oregon Department of Revenue shall submit an annual report to the House Committee on Revenue and the Senate Committee on Finance and Revenue beginning February 1, 2027.

    (2) The report shall include:

    (a) An estimate of total tax revenue forgone as a result of this Act.

    (b) An estimate of the number of minor taxpayers affected.

    (c) An analysis of the economic and social impact of the policy.

    (d) Any recommended revisions or adjustments to the exemption.


    SECTION 7. Severability Clause.
    If any provision of this Act, or the application thereof to any person or circumstance, is found to be invalid, the remainder of this Act and the application of its provisions to other persons or circumstances shall not be affected thereby.


    SECTION 8. Effective Date.
    This Act shall take effect on the 91st day after the date on which the 2025 regular session of the Legislative Assembly adjourns sine die.
